# ROLE 3 — Transportation & Traffic

> You own the spatial substrate of the city. Every agent's movement happens on your network.

## The big picture

The transport network is what makes this an urban simulation rather than a generic crowd simulation. Roads, intersections, metro lines, bus routes — these define what's possible, where congestion forms, where stress accumulates. When the user watches rain spread and sees red bloom across a map of commute times, that visual is your subsystem speaking.

You handle two distinct but coupled problems: **routing** (given an agent at A wanting to reach B, what's the best path right now?) and **flow** (given many agents using the same segments, how does that affect speed and occupancy?). Solving either alone is straightforward. Solving them together at scale is where it gets interesting.

## What you own

- The **multi-modal network graph** (roads, metro, bus) — its representation and maintenance
- The **routing service** — answering "best path" queries fast enough for many agents per tick
- The **congestion model** — how speed degrades as flow increases
- **Vehicle and station occupancy** — how full each road, bus, and metro car is
- **Traffic light or signal logic** if the team decides to model it
- **Network response to events** — what happens when a road closes, a metro line stops, capacity changes

## Mental models you need

**A transport network is two graphs glued together.** The physical graph (roads as edges, intersections as nodes) and the demand graph (origin-destination pairs, generated by agents). Your job is to mediate between them — physical conditions shape what's possible, demand shapes what's stressed.

**Congestion is a feedback loop, not a static map.** More agents on a segment → slower travel → some agents reroute → new segments load up → equilibrium emerges. This is the heart of the BPR-style volume-delay relationship. Get this loop right and your maps will look real. Skip it and the city looks dead even at 200,000 agents.

**Speed is not optional.** If routing takes 200ms per query and you have 10,000 agents each needing 2 queries per day, that's 4,000 seconds just in routing per simulated day. Cache aggressively. Precompute where you can. Use the cheapest algorithm that gives acceptable answers.

**Real road graphs are messy.** OSM data for Indian cities will have one-way streets tagged wrong, dangling edges, mislabeled road classes, and parts of the city missing. You will spend more time cleaning the graph than building the routing algorithm. Plan for it.

**A bad routing algorithm can be saved by good caching. A bad network representation cannot be saved by anything.** Spend your design time on the graph structure.

## Key decisions you'll make

- How is the network represented internally — adjacency list, edge list, something specialized?
- What routing algorithm? Dijkstra is safe; A* is faster with a heuristic; contraction hierarchies are very fast but complex to implement. How fast do you actually need to be?
- How are travel times computed — free-flow with multipliers, BPR function, or something else?
- How do you represent multi-modal trips (walk → metro → walk)? A unified graph, or coordination between mode-specific graphs?
- How granular is congestion tracked — per edge, per edge per time-bucket, something finer?
- Do you model individual vehicles (microscopic) or just flows (mesoscopic / macroscopic)? Mesoscopic is usually right for this scale.

## Interfaces with other roles

- **Data Engineering (Role 4)** delivers the cleaned road network and transit definitions to you
- **Agent Behavior (Role 2)** queries you for routes and travel time estimates; the demand they generate becomes your load
- **Simulation Core (Role 1)** ticks you each step so you can update congestion state
- **Frontend (Role 6)** consumes your edge-level speeds and station-level occupancy for heatmap rendering
- **Research (Role 7)** wants commute time distributions, mode-specific travel times, and congestion patterns as outputs

## What "done" looks like

- Routing queries return in well under your per-tick budget at target population
- Congestion responds visibly and plausibly to load — peak hours look like peak hours
- The network handles "what if this link closes?" cleanly and the impact is observable
- Multi-modal trips (walk + metro + walk) work end-to-end
- A teammate can swap in a different city's network without rewriting your core code

## Anti-patterns to avoid

- Running fresh Dijkstra for every agent every tick
- Modeling each individual car when flows would suffice
- Treating the metro and bus networks as black boxes (they need to congest too)
- Ignoring the data cleaning phase and assuming OSM "just works"
- Building beautiful routing for cars but ignoring pedestrian and transit links
- Over-investing in microscopic detail when the rest of the team is mesoscopic

## Open questions you'll need to think through

- How do you handle agents already in transit when their route gets worse (congestion ahead)? Do they re-route mid-trip?
- What's the right granularity for the congestion model — per-minute? per-tick? hourly buckets?
- Should metro and bus capacity be hard limits (passengers refused if full) or soft (comfort penalty)?
- How do you avoid the trap of computing a separate route for every agent when many agents share origin-destination pairs?
- For the demo: do you visualize specific vehicles, aggregate flow on roads, or both?
